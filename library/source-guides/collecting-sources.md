# How to collect good sources

The quality of your teaching materials depends on the quality of your sources.
This guide helps you put together a folder that an AI can turn into good
materials. It is written for anyone: you do not need to be technical.

## Why this matters

If your sources are thin, out of date, or contradict each other, the materials
the AI makes will have the same problems. A little care here saves a lot of
editing later.

## What to put in

Aim to include a mix of:

- **Core explanations.** Clear writing that explains the topic.
- **Examples.** Worked examples show how the idea is used.
- **Activities and questions.** Practice the AI can learn the style from.
- **Assessment material.** Mark schemes or success criteria, if you have them.
- **Learner-level information.** A note of who this is for and their level.
- **Curriculum or exam requirements.** If your teaching must match a syllabus.

## What to leave out

- **Duplicates.** The same file twice can skew the result.
- **Out-of-date files.** Old versions confuse the picture.
- **Private student data.** Never include names, contact details, ID numbers,
  or anything that identifies a real learner. Remove or anonymise it first.
- **Copyrighted material you may not use.** Only include material you have
  permission to use.
- **Background reading you will not teach from.** Wide reading that is not
  about your topic adds noise.

## A simple way to start

1. Make one folder.
2. Put your best three to ten files in it.
3. Make sure at least one file explains the idea and at least one gives an
   example.
4. Remove anything private.
5. Build the content pack and run the readiness check. It will tell you what is
   missing.

## File types that work

Markdown Mentor can read text, Markdown, Word, PDF, PowerPoint, HTML, and CSV
files, as well as ZIP files containing them. If a file will not convert, save
it as a Word document or a PDF and try again.
